In summary, of the top 6 species examined across time of day and fishing depths we found that Cynoscion virescens and Macrodon ancylodon were often the largest discarded species whereas Stellifer rastrifer and Bagre bagre were often the smallest discarded species. Four of the top six species measured (i.e.Â Macrodon ancylodon, Nebris microps, Bagre bagre and Cynoscion virescens are commercially viable to the Atlantic seabob and other local fisheries in Guyana. Despite most of the discarded species being juveniles (tiny) and below the common economic length for landing i.e.Â 25cm, there were some observations of the discarded fish species which were of marketable lengths. 4.2.2 Analysis of the mean lengths for discarded fish species across time of day and fishing depths using the Welch Two Sample t-test.

Test assumptions

This test assumes that the the length observations across time of day (day/night) and fishing depths (deep/shallow) are normally distributed. Fortunately as well, this method is not at all sensitive to deviations from this assumption. Therefore, if the distributions of day/night or deep/shallow are the same e.g.Â if both distributions are skewed to the right/left. As seen from the histograms (Figures 4.6 and 4.7), the length distribution fits this expectation nicely. Therefore, the analysis will be performed on the actual length variable and no transformation will be required. As a precautionary measure, this assumption was also tested in chapter 3.2.2 and proven to be accurate

How the Welch Two Sample t-test works

The test statistic, \(t_s\), is computed using a method that has the difference between the means in the numerator. This therefore makes the \(t_s\) larger as the means move away from each other. The standard error of the difference in the means in this case is denominator. This value decreases as the sample variances decreases or if the size of sample increase. Hence the \(t_s\) gets larger as the means move further apart, the variances get smaller, or if the size of the sample increase. The probability of getting the observed \(t_s\) value under the null hypothesis is computed using the t-distribution.

Conducting the t-tests
